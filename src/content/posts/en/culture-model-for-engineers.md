If the [business model](/posts/business-model-for-engineers/) is a business's "how it earns," the **culture model is an org's "how it moves."**
A culture model is the org blueprint that pushes stated values all the way down into **behaviors and systems**. To an executive,
culture is not "an atmosphere that forms on its own" but **something to deliberately design and maintain**. As an engineer's second
step toward an executive view, this post lays out that way of thinking.

## What a culture model is — values into behaviors and systems

"Our culture values taking on challenges" — stop there and it's just a slogan. A culture model connects a value to
**concrete behaviors**, and further to **the systems that support them**, in one line.

| Layer | Content | Example ("value challenge") |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Value | What you hold dear | Challenge; don't blame failure |
| Behavior | How it shows up day to day | Try small / share failures |
| System | The mechanism that supports it | Failure retros; evaluation that protects psychological safety |

> ⭐ When values and systems conflict, culture always **loses to the system side.** Say "take on challenges" while punishing
> failure in reviews, and no one will. **People follow the behavior that gets rewarded, not the words on the wall.** This is what executives watch most.

## Why "design" it — leave it alone and it decays

Culture arises even if you neglect it. But it **won't necessarily be the culture you want.** Executives design it deliberately
because leaving it to spontaneity produces three things.

- **Dilutes at scale**: at 10 people a founder's example carries the culture; at 100 it vanishes unless articulated and systematized
- **Bad culture also arises on its own**: blame-shifting and don't-rock-the-boat attitudes take root the more you neglect them
- **Hiring drifts**: without articulating what "a fit" means, the org's character scatters the more you grow

> 💡 The point of writing culture down isn't "a motto on the wall" — it's to **align the judgment criteria for hiring, evaluation, and decisions.**
> With a shared yardstick to return to when in doubt, the org moves the same direction without the executive intervening at every turn.

## What it's made of — where culture lives

A culture model works only once it's embedded in **daily mechanisms**, not chanted. The main places it embeds:

- **Hiring**: who you let in / don't. Culture is decided most strongly by "who you make a teammate"
- **Evaluation & rewards**: what you reward decides the behavior actually rewarded = the real culture (stronger than words)
- **Decision-making**: who decides and how (top-down vs. delegated to the field). How you place authority is culture itself
- **Onboarding / information openness**: how you convey it to new members, how far you open up information

> 🧭 In engineer terms, a culture model is close to an org's **"lint and CI."** Rather than leaving values (the coding convention)
> to goodwill, you **protect them with a mechanism (automated checks).** Systematizing means making desired behavior "easy to pass"
> and undesired behavior "hard to pass" — building a mechanism where good behavior naturally increases.

## Meshing it with the business model

A culture model means nothing on its own. It becomes a force only as a **culture that fits the business's way of earning.**

| Business model | Example of a meshing culture |
| --- | --- |
| A startup iterating fast | Quick decisions, failure tolerance, authority delegated to the field |
| Payments/infra where trust is everything | Caution, review-first, a [build-quality-in](/posts/qa-quality-built-in/) stance |
| A business earning via large-scale support | Customer-first, care, standardization |

> ⚠️ A culture mismatched with the business breeds friction. Put "ship then fix" into a trust-critical business and incidents rise;
> impose heavy approvals on a fast-iterating business and it can't move. **Choose culture to follow business strategy** — don't copy trends.

## Summary

- A culture model is the org blueprint that pushes values **down into behaviors and systems.** Don't stop at a slogan
- When values and systems conflict, **the system wins.** People follow **rewarded behavior**, not words on the wall
- Culture arises even neglected, but not in the shape you want. Design it to prevent **dilution at scale, spontaneous bad culture, and hiring drift**
- Culture lives in **hiring, evaluation, decision-making, and openness.** In engineer terms it's close to "lint and CI" — protected by mechanism
- **Choose culture to fit the business model.** A mismatched culture breeds friction. Don't copy trends
